1. Introduction

 

Welcome, the following information will explain in detail how to claim land and property legally.

The UK Land Registry currently holds details of around 21 million registered land plots and properties; in effect it’s a massive database of property ownership guaranteed by the land registry itself.

In simple terms, this leaves an estimated 7 million unregistered land plot and properties in the UK right now with a combined value of billions of £’s.

This is not to say all 7 million can easily be claimed … But taking into account the size of the figures involved here, the likelihood of finding several plots of land and properties that you could claim is very good indeed.

According to the Empty Homes Agency there are approximately 900,000 empty homes in Britain of which nearly 800,000 are in London. A sizeable proportion of these are abandoned or derelict.

These properties, few of which have traceable owners, are just the type that after carrying out all proper research you may be able to claim from using this guide.

These properties will of course at some time have had an owner. It may be that the owner has simply never registered his or her title…

However it could also be that the owner has died and left no will and has no relatives or that the owner has never moved away from the area many years ago effectively abandoning the property.

These then briefly are some of the types of property and the plots. Which by the following the detailed guidelines within this guide, you could claim as your own via the legal vehicle of adverse possession. We will cover all these points in much more detail later.

Another interesting point worthy of mentioning here is the Royal Institute of Chartered Surveyors figure of over 17,000 plots of land and property that are being illegally claimed every year or around 250 per week.

Not all of these would be claims of the scale and type we are going to consider in this guide. However this did lead Charles Harpum of the Law Commission (who helped draft the recent Land registration Act 2002) to recently comment that people claiming land and property had become something of a “national pastime”.

The legal definition of adverse possession is as follows.

“The gaining of title real estate is continuous hostile, open, and notorious possession over a statutory period”. Again we’ll cover the content of this definition in further detail, as we progress.

Initially, the land plots and properties you’ll be looking for could be run down or abandoned…Derelict farmhouses or disowned buildings…

Properties accessed via an overgrown driveway & rusty gates … Fields, hidden plots of land…. Even old terraced or detached houses.

2. Step 1: Getting Started

 

Strange as it may sound the best place to start lies on your own doorstep.

Take the time to walk around your particular area. If you are a car owner and make regular short journeys, try walking from time to time. It's amazing the different things you will notice ... the things you would never see if you were driving.

Use your keen eye to spot things you never knew existed. Does a bit of land and property you see on your journey look well maintained?

There's sure to be some suitable land or property in your vicinity ... That broken down house? Derelict farmhouse An overgrown plot of land? An abandoned property?

Keep your eyes peeled, because you never know when the first opportunity is going to happen. It most certainly will eventually. Watchful eye.

The best land plots and properties that are worth considering for future claims are the ones that appear overgrown, un-kept or unused. This type can be classified into the following categories ...

However, this is not an exhaustive list, and you may wish to take time to consider any further categories which you feel may be worthy.

A: Plots of land that is empty and overgrown with vegetation

B: Plots of land that are vacant and wild flowers occur with trees and rubble.

C: Land used by an adjacent landowner on an unofficial basis.

D: Empty plots, which are neither agricultural nor arable land.

E: Land with no evident or well defined boundaries, which seems unkempt and vacant, together with its boundaries.

F: Land that includes a derelict building located on it, which appears to be weakened and in a poor state of repair.

G: Properties/factories/buildings in poor state of repair and vacant.

H: A combination of every one of one or more of the above.

Keep an open mind and use your imagination. All information listed above should set alarm bells ringing. When searching for suitable target properties and land plots to claim, make sure to keep in mind the following points at all times.

Do not be too quick to assume that empty pieces of land are owned by the local authority, electrical or water companies.... Especially when they are located in built-up areas.

Do not automatically assume that either the local authority or the adjacent land owners own all grassed verges.

Just because animals are pasturing a piece of land it does not mean that it is owned or. Registered.

It may be worth looking for land that is occasionally occupied by travelers or Gypsies, as this land may be suitable.

Do not automatically assume that land being used as someone's garden is automatically owned by them. As you may be aware there many disputes between adjoining land owners where boundaries have been relocated so as to gain land from someone else.

Do not assume that land or properties which are used for any particular purpose are in. fact owned.

Do not assume that all canal banks and the national waterways authority owns. Footpaths. This is not always the case.

In summary never assume anything, instead always assess the facts and make your own judgment if no one appears to be using the land or property, then it may be worth further investigation and review

Since we have located our "prospect" we think could be suitable for claiming through adverse possession, the next crucial stage is the making of investigations.

I can not overemphasize how crucial this stage is and how important it is to make diligent and careful investigation to discover as much about the site as possible, and more importantly for our purposes whether it is registered unregistered and/or are there identifiable and traceable owners.

Also, again please remember the fact a property or land is unregistered is NOT definite confirmation the land or property may be claimed or is not owned ... Every situation and circumstance needs careful, prudent and diligent investigation.

Our starting point here is the land registry and we can access the new electronic online search facility which is located at... http://www.landregistry.gov.uk/

7. If the property is registered

 

This simply means that someone at sometime has been registered as the owner of the property in question.

The time of the last update of the register provides us with some idea of how recently someone had “active” ownership of the property or land in question, although of course this is not conclusive of their interest now.

The land could still be unwanted, disowned or part of a deceased estate and a claim may still be a possibility.

 

8. The land or property is unregistered

 

Please do not make the mistake of automatically assuming that land which is unregistered and unkempt is available.

It is fact that all land even unregistered is owned by someone somewhere.

If, however, you have carried out full and proper research and extensive investigations into the property and it appears unused, unwanted and disowned with any reputed owners untraceable, then this may be a distinct possibility for staking a claim to.

Please note

You should consider carefully if the copy of the register or title plan has revealed any specific rights of way, covenants or restrictions, and/or any rights it has over surrounding properties.

These will not crop up very often but if they do appear then you should seek clarification from a solicitor or alternatively your local council-planning department may be of assistance.

This is due to the fact that if anybody has a legal right of way over and/or across a significant part of the land in question, then (according to my recent feedback) the land registry would class your adverse possession claim as “highly unlikely”.

You would of course, have to weigh the facts of the matter, but in the majority of cases your ultimate claim for adverse possession would not be granted.

For specific guidance in relation to this point please consult with your solicitor in the first instance. It also goes without saying that erecting a “NO TRESPASSING” sign, where a right of way currently exists would be of little relevance.

16. Tracing the owner or Last known owner

 

Irrespective of whether your target land or property is REGISTERED or unregistered, you must always take the necessary steps in order to try to locate track down the actual owner.

Methods to track down and locate the owner as follows:

Enquire with people in the immediate vicinity as to the last owner. The last owner may have died or had no relatives. In the case of a house, nobody may have visited it for many years.

Make some discreet enquiries at neighbouring properties about the owner-­ or if any one tends to the land or gardens etc.

Check the voting register/electoral roll for the specific locality in question. This is usually available for inspection at your local library or alternatively you could visit www.192.com and carry out an electronic search of the register nationwide.

You will need to register, and there is a charge for searching, however you can easily obtain free search credits by completing various online questions from sponsors.

Chat to the landlord of the local public house and bring up the land or property in general conversation.

Ask at the local post office whether they recall having delivered any mail to the address in question and if so when.

Ask the reactor or vicar of the local church.

Enquire with the local garage, petrol station newsagents, shop keeper or milkman etc.

By asking local people, and immediate neighbours if they know the exact whereabouts of the owner (tell them you are trying to locate the owner with a view to buying the land or property) you could probably get to know where he or she is... Whether or not they are deceased… The year or approximate year they died… Whether or not anyone has visited the property since they died… Or whether they had any relations etc.

At the end of the day, you really will be amazed at the amount of detailed information you could unearth by simply speaking to the relevant people.

Note: You can assign the task of searching for a specific person (or a will) to a specialized company such as www.1stlocate.com although there would be a charge for this.

Now should you fail to discover any relevant information in relation to the owner, or last known owner, or the consensus of the opinions you receive lead you to believe the owner has in fact died, then you will need to check this out further via your local probate registry office to discover whether a will was made. Further information concerning how to achieve this can be found online....... at http://www.probate.co.uk/advice/topic.asp?id=445

This is quite a difficult site to find your way around... useful links below. http://www.justice.gov.uk/courts/probate Please note: the addresses of the local District Registries should you wish to search in person (as opposed to making a postal search) are at the bottom of the page.

Some further useful information can be found at..... http://www.justice.gov.uk/courts/probate/copies-of-grants-wills

There is also a down loadable printable “Application for Probate Search” forms in pdf format available at...... http://www.justice.gov.uk/forms?query=Application+for+Probate+Search&collection=moj-matrix-dev-forms&form=simple&profile=_default

In general, providing you have the minimum details of the owners name, and (estimated) year of death, this check should enable you to confirm either way, whether or not the owner has died, and if so whether they left a will, and if the land or property in question was actually left to anyone.

As you will note inquires can be made via post, or by paying a personal visit to the post office.

If your inquiries at the UK probate registry confirm that the owner has died then you may be able to stake your claim to the land or property, as you may find that the beneficiaries have either forgotten about the land or there are no beneficiaries...

Or the beneficiaries cannot be traced. Obviously this is time consuming so you will need to do you own diligence into the beneficiaries, to see if they are in fact using the land or property.

There are simple steps that you must take prior to the commencement of any particular land plot or property claim via adverse possession (is it Registered or Unregistered)…

Because ideally you do not want the owner to appear straight after you take possession of the land or property. This will just be a total waste of time and effort....

So do your homework.

Now, armed with your newly acquired information (or lack of information), you’ll now need to assess your next move.

If the owner is still living and there is a good chance of your adverse possession claim being quickly challenged, then you should strike this land or property off your target list and look elsewhere.

As I have stated before in this guide there are just so many claimable land plots and properties out there.

You needn’t waste your valuable time and effort on one like this. At this point you should revert back to earlier in this guide and start the action rolling again.

However, if your inquiries lead you to believe that there is no known traceable owner, or the owner is deceased, or not left a will, or they have moved away...

In other words, not likely the owner will turn up at a later date, and then is time to stake your claim.

 

17. How to start legal process of claiming the property via adverse possession

 

An introduction and overview of adverse Possession:

We have all heard that statement "Possession is nine tenths of the law". Well this is an important principle used on a daily basis within the land and property sector. It also the same legal statement that will enable you to claim land or plots and properties, virtually for free.

Also keep in mind that by "rescuing" abandoned & land plots and properties, you are in effect performing a service to society and yourself.

Why? Because the abandoned properties become a blight on the community. They can quickly become a haven for undesirables, a rendezvous for drug suppliers, a danger to children, fire hazard, and an eyesore that drags down surrounding property values.

Now moving on from there....

Generally the land Registry accepts that if you have taken possession of a land plot (or property) for a statutory amount of time... Currently twelve years for unregistered or ten years for registered land/property, under the current legislation and without any objection being raised by either the owner or a counter claimant, then when it come to unregistered land/property ... You may ultimately become the Registered Owner of the land or property with Possessory Freehold Title.

In the case of the 10 years' continuous adverse possession of registered land/property. Then you will be granted transfer of title, pro- rata to the previous owner’s title. That is to. Say if the previous owners' title class was absolute, then you would be granted Absolute. Title.

This, obviously, would ultimately be decided upon following the Land Registry's attempt to contact the previous registered owner after the ten year period had elapsed ... And without receiving a reply back from the previous registered owner within the prescribed time. This point is covered in further detail later within this guide.

However here's the bottom line:

The fact that you did not buy the land or property concerned, and you did not inherit it, makes no difference whatsoever in the eyes of the law.

Furthermore, dependant upon the power of your adverse possession claim, you may be able to have your current Possessory freehold title on unregistered converted to absolute freehold title, shortly after the statutory twelve year period, shortly after you have been registered as the owner and been granted Possessory Freehold Title to the land.

Again, this topic we be fully covered as we progress via this guide.

Before we get too far ahead of ourselves, let's take a look at the way in which you should proceed in order to get your actual adverse possession claim underway.

The next sections of this guide, namely: 'Laying claim ... The guideline and Effective statutory Period usage deal in great detail with the various ways which you should proceed to claim your specific land plot or property, and more importantly, the specific activities you may and may not undertake during your statutory ten or twelve years of occupation.

The guidelines are in line with the land Registry's current legislation, and will therefore present you with the best possible opportunity of successfully claiming the land plot or property as your very own and achieving your ultimate goal of being granted Absolute Title to it.

Laying claim...

Although you are not required to submit any forms to the land registry at this initial stage, it is good to keep some relevant documentary evidence to backup the start date of your claim, by following the next steps.

Draw up a basic statement along the lines of:

"Date/time

I am commencing my claim via adverse possession, of land/property located at ...Then the highlighted attached plans.

Have two people sign and date your statement then file this document away for safe keeping. You may also wish to take several photographs of the land/property in question, with yourself in the picture. Note this can be saved for your own personal use.

No forms needed at this stage? Why you ask? Simple, because the land registry are not even slightly concerned about you or your adverse possession claim until the prescribed ten year period for registration or twelve years for unregistered property has elapsed.

Now after you've filed away your documentary evidence, and with regard to initially commencing your claim and physically finding yourself in adverse possession of the land, here's what you should do next.
