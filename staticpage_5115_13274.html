18. Erect a fence around the land area

 

Obviously if the area is large this could be expensive when it comes to planning permission the current law for the whole of the UK states as follows:

You can build a fence (or a wall) around a specific area of land, but should the fence or wall exceed two metres in height, planning permission from the local authority will be needed.

Where the frontage of the proposed fence or wall is directly adjacent to a road or a highway, the maximum permitted height of the fence or wall is 1 metre. The erection of any fence or wall can not be allowed to affect visibility of road users and/or affect the visibility of any pre-determined access point.

In summary, you can make an application for planning permission irrespective of regardless if you. Own the land or property. You should also check the current ruling with your local council planning department just to make doubly sure.

With regard to the land Registry, Adverse possession and the important subject of fencing, the Land registry has clear guidelines briefly detailed as follows.

"Where the land was previously open ground, fencing is strong evidence of factual possession, but is neither indispensable nor conclusive".

This simply means that partitioning the area is recommended.

Make the land looks presentable by cutting the grass trimming the hedges and removing any rubbish etc.

Put up a gate to keep intruders out. If the land you are claiming is used by people in the immediate vicinity as common land or waste ground, the erecting of a fence or wall around the land area may well be your best option. Padlock the gates also. In short, treat the property as your own

Let’s now have a look at what you must actually achieve with the land plot in order to fully. Adhere to the respective adverse possession legislation.

Land statutory Period Usage...

For your land or property claim to become successful, your usage must be...

\* Exclusive.

\* Permanent.

\* Uninterrupted.

\* Unchallenged.

And all of these must make an application for the current minimum statutory period of ten years (for registered land/property) or twelve years (for unregistered land).

To further elaborate on these extremely important points, please note the following three direct quotes from the current Land Registry leaflets....

What is required is not an intention to own or perhaps even an intention to acquire ownership, but an intention to possess. This means the intention in one's name and on one's own behalf, to exclude the world at large, including the owner with the proper appear title if he be not himself the possessor, up until now as reasonably practicable therefore far as the processes of the law will allow.

No action shall be brought be anyone to recover any land after the expiration of twelve years from the date on which the right of action accrued to him or, if it first accrued to some individual through whom he claims, to that person the right of action accrues therefore the limitation period starts to operate from the start of the adverse possession.

An existing owner loses his land or her ownership (whether it be of registered or unregistered land) after there has been "uninterrupted adverse possession for the relevant period" laid down in the limitation act 1980.

So there you have it. Providing that during the statutory period, ten to twelve years of your adverse possession, no person else has proved that they are the owner and you can prove to the land registry that you have been using or have developed use (permanently and on-going) of the land or property for the statutory period, being granted ownership with possessory freehold title should not be a problem...

Or having the title transferred pro-rata to the previous registered owner’s title, when it comes to registration land or property.

The only exclusion to the statutory period is relevant to crown land, where statutory period is never less than thirty years.

During your occupation of the land (preceding making an application for ownership and/or transfer of title) there are many ways whereby you can also generate an income from the land, which consequently will go a long way to providing your permanent and on-going usage and occupation of it.

20. Building the land

 

After TWELVE years continuous possession of the unregistered land, and your successful registration of ownership with possessory Freehold title (or title transfers pro-rata to the previous registered owners title), you will have no trouble whatsoever with building on (or developing) the land, planning permission permitting, obviously.

Should you decide to erect a property, or fully repair/renovate the existing property before the conclusion of the statutory period, then any improvements you make will be conveyed to the land owner or their relatives, should they unexpectedly appear and prove their ownership. From personal experience, I would advise you not to build anything on the land during statutory period.

23. Repairs, Renovations & Utility Issues

 

If the property is in a run down state, then you'll have to take care of this accordingly. Your ultimate objective here is to secure the property into a position where you'll be able to physically enter and live there, or alternatively rent the property out to a tenant.

These options are covered in detail later within this guide.

Until your claim has gone the statutory ten or twelve years distance and you have been granted possessory Freehold title (or had the previous owners title transferred to you) you would be unwise to spend any great amount of money on the refurbishment.

For instance, spending thousands of pounds on a new kitchen or bathroom might be money wasted at this very beginning in the proceedings, as there is always a chance that the true owner could appear at any moment during the statutory period.

Inexpensive repairs should not be a problem, i.e. painting walls, floors, etc.

These are the options you should seriously consider prior to deciding to enter there yourself or renting the property out.

Some properties may well only need a lick of paint and a good tidy up, others may need more attention. You should obviously, take these factors into account during the early stages of your investigations into properties you could claim adverse possession.

Services to the property will also need careful assessment and consideration.

25. Being granted Absolute title:

 

Taking the brief overview into account would probably lead you to believe that you must wait 24 years before being granted Absolute Title to the land or property.

Based on the letter of the law this is true, but in reality you could make an application for, and be granted Absolute title long before then. Here is how.....

The Law....

Where the title is a possessory one, whether freehold or leasehold, the proprietor is entitled to request conversion to either absolute freehold or good leasehold if the land has been registered for twelve years.

Application can also be made at any time after the land is first registered, to convert a possessory freehold to absolute.

Which means after twelve years you can convert to absolute? Extra 1-2 years from your claim date, before the 24 years have elapsed.

Worse case scenario is that you make an application for conversion of title and it is rejected. There would be no harm done, and you will have to wait the full term whilst still proceeding to benefit from the land or property in the meantime.

Along with the above point, do not forget that you still have the opportunity to cash in via Defective title indemnity insurance plan, as outlined earlier within this guide.

Statutory Declaration.

This should be completed with the aid of your solicitor, to include and cover all the relevant details applicable to your claim, including the following information.

The circumstances through which your adverse possession started, including specific date.

The specific purpose for which the land or property has been used and the activities conducted there.

The extent of which the land or property has been used and the activities carried on. there.

If there is a gate, details regarding, if there is a lock and key, and if so, who holds the key?

The name and address of the owner or the person thought to be the owner.

The title number and documentation of any registered land you may own that adjoins the land or property area you are claiming.

Confirmation that your occupation of the land or property has not been under a lease, tenancy, licence, or with the consent of anyone.

Full details and documentation relating to any disputes arising from your occupation (or a tenant's occupation) of the land or property.

The forms FR-I and DL should be brought to a practicing land law solicitor, who will help you complete them in line with your objective. A plan of the land area should also be affixed to form FR-I, with the respective acreage highlighted.

Your solicitor will also be required to sign the form and sign and witness your statutory declaration. Form DL should also be returned to the land registry in duplicate. Two copies should be sent together with the rest of the forms and documentation.

All this may seem a lot to absorb at a beginning, but keep in mind this will not be needed until twelve years' time..... Your solicitor will be knowledgeable with these forms.

Finally when it comes to the Fee for the FR-J submission, this is based upon the value of the property. Your best option here would be to estimate it yourself between 0 and £50,000.

Land registry fee of £40. The only exception is if a surveyor needs to visit the site in relation to a land dispute or court hearing, a fee will apply in this particular instance.

After submitting your forms and your statutory declaration and assuming everything is 100 percent, you should hear back from them with confirmation. File the document and store in a safe place.

Shortly after submission of your ADV-I and supporting evidence here's what will happen next....

The Land registry will contact the registered owner of the land property, the person they currently include on their records as being the registered owner, to advise them that they have received an official application to have the land or property registered in the name of the new owner i.e. you.

It will, of course, be worded very differently to that, but that will be the main communication.

Upon receipt of the letter, the registered owner of the land or property will have a maximum of sixty five business days, three months so as to respond to the communication and object to the registration, if this happens then naturally your application will be rejected.

Again, it is because of this that before proceeding with an adverse possession claim you should make sure to carry out all checks.

Now if all checks out ok, you should hear back from communication with confirmation of your registered ownership.

If for any reason you were unsuccessful then you are entitled to apply again after two years.
